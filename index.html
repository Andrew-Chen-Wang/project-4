Bitcoin Replacing Cash

Analyzing the progress cryptocurrency has made in replacing cash.

By: Andrew Chen Wang
Get Started

Introduction

Steve Jobs first introduced the Macintosh in 1984. It was initially a flop, but, as time progressed, new innovations helped innovate the next generation of computers to the point that we all carry on in our pockets.

Bitcoin is a cryptocurrency using blockchain technology. It decentralized money such that no government backs the currency (e.g. the US has a Federal Reserve bank that helps manage the USD value).

Our question: can cryptocurrency, specifically Bitcoin, replace cash as we know it?

Use Cases of Bitcoin

Before we can answer "what can you do with Bitcoin?", let's first talk about what we can do with cash.

You can buy a stroller

or some cafeteria food.

How's Bitcoin any different? Well let's see: you can buy...

a stroller

or some cafeteria food.

There's essentially no difference.

So why haven't we changed yet? Bitcoin is just a different currency from cash. Not many people own even a little bit of Bitcoin. This may be due to how expensive it is to get your first one or how inconvenient it is to setup a wallet to grab some from your local bank. Bitcoin is simply a tech craze that's bubbled into market analyzers' eyes in recent years. We're currently seeing art auctions taking place with Bitcoin and some apps and ATMs being created to assist those who don't know where to begin in owning some Bitcoin.

Overtime we may see more ways to use Bitcoin such as buying a stroller or buying food from anywhere you want (with the added bonus of no international currency exchanges). The problem lies in adoption.

In other words, if not enough of us use Bitcoin, there's no new use case (i.e. Target ain't going to bother having cashiers accept Bitcoin for your stroller). Thus, the less trees, the less oxygen for Bitcoin to breathe.

Shades of Wealth

Which hill caught your attention first? Let's call that the "Rich People Hill" where they hide all their money to evade taxes.

Each shade represents a bracket of our global population and wealth distribution. Although not realistically representative, it's satisfactory to say that one of the hills represents the people who can either afford or find a way to use Bitcoin today.

How about the other hills? The first hill you immediately looked at needs to invest in helping the other hills shine. The only reason you saw the first hill is because of its distinguishing glamor and fashion sense. Or mostly because it had a shade of black more appealing to your eyes. If the first hill were to globally invest in stores, then the other hills can have similar features as the first hill. The same can be said of Bitcoin. With more investment into its expansion, then Bitcoin could become more useful to the general global population.

This isn't to say that the first hill won't remain glamorous. Investment means returns.

The catch?

It's not that black and white

Everything needs investing. But without returns, there's no incentive to invest. What's given resources first? Who's willing to dump quintillions into developing ways Nepal and Luxembourg can own a phone and do some Bitcoin transactions? Some places don't have the required internet access to conduct a transaction (~80% of the predominantly black South African neighborhood of Nonzamo don't have access compared to Strand, the predominantly white South African neighborhood).

Even if we just started in the US with widespread internet access, stores like Walmart need to train cashiers on how to deal with Bitcoin transactions. New services like credit would have to be reworked to accommodate for the mostly anonymous, cryptographically unhackable blockchain technology that runs Bitcoin.
